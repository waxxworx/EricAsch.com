About

Where I'm
coming from

Over ten years ago when I was in my forties, I finally got sober. It wasn't because I hit rock bottom — it was because I was able to see my mess reflected in the faces of my sons, then 13 and 10. It was at a family-friendly music festival, and back then festival equated to "all day drinking." Saturday didn't end well. I was a mess. But in the fallout from that weekend, I was sickened to think that I might be anything less than my best for my boys. So I stopped drinking.

It just so happened that around the same time, my oldest son and I got involved in a rites of passage organization for young men. We were seeking an initiation experience for him that I hadn't had. Connecting with that community led us both into men's work — his growth, then his brother's, my own development as a facilitator and leader. And on and on. That journey is still going.

Then life kept happening. Nine years ago — this year — I lost my mom. A few years after that, I lost a job I'd built much of my identity around. Grief, failure, disorientation. What I found in those years was that the tools I'd already been gathering — meditation, men's work, plant medicine, honest community — weren't just nice practices for the good times. They were the things that actually held me together when things fell apart. Loss and failure as forging fire. I mean that literally, not as a bumper sticker.

These days I'm exploring how to introduce others to the transformative tools I've learned along the way. I'm completing my certification as a mindfulness meditation teacher through the program with Tara Brach and Jack Kornfield — not because I've arrived anywhere, but because I want to do this work well and with integrity. I teach at Sacred Roots in Charlotte and online.

I also work in corporate technology and teach graduate courses in AI ethics. I've been with my wife for 29 years, married for 24. I love being outdoors, traveling, watching movies, and going deep on music — medicine music, EDM, the kind of sound that moves something in you. I don't live like a monk. I live like a person who is trying to pay attention, and who thinks that's actually worth doing.

If any of that resonates — if you're somewhere in the middle of your own forging fire, or just feeling called to go a little deeper — I'd love to connect.
